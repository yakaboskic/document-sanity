\abstract{
Human genetic evidence is known to increase the likelihood of clinical success in drug development \cite{ochoa_human_2022,rusina_genetic_2023}. Yet fewer than 5\% of active drug programs are supported by germline genetic data \cite{minikel_refining_2024}, revealing a critical gap in how genetic knowledge is incorporated into therapeutic pipelines. To help bridge this gap, we introduce the concept of indirect genetic support, which captures evidence derived from associations with biologically related genes linked through shared annotations. This expands on direct genetic support by probabilistically integrating both genetic and non-genetic information into a unified model aimed at improving mechanistic understanding and clinical outcomes. We present PIGEAN, a Bayesian framework that quantifies direct and indirect support using GWAS summary statistics, exome-wide gene-level associations, and rare disease gene sets. Applied across 6,000 complex traits, 2,000 rare diseases, 600,000 gene annotations, and 29,000 drug programs, PIGEAN constructs a weighted graph that serves as a genetically grounded backbone for biomedical discovery. Our analysis shows that drug targets prioritized via this graph achieve clinical success rates comparable to those found in curated drug target databases, while also revealing over 1,500 additional viable targets through indirect evidence alone. By organizing gene prioritization along the interpretable axes of direct and indirect support, PIGEAN offers a principled alternative to existing ad hoc methods. When paired with AI agents, this framework enables scalable, mechanistically informed exploration of the gene–phenotype landscape, probablistically connecting every gene to every trait using the structure of current biological knowledge.
}

\section{Main}\label{sec:introduction}

% Broad introductory paragraph
Most human traits are influenced by many genes, and most human genes influence many traits. Mapping this complex web of gene–trait relationships is essential for uncovering disease biology \cite{cohen_sequence_2006}, understanding clinical heterogeneity \cite{udler_type_2018}, accurately diagnosing patients \cite{yang_clinical_2013}, and designing effective therapies \cite{plenge_validating_2013}. Among the most widely used and powerful approaches to to identify gene-trait relationships are genetic association studies. For common traits, genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified tens of thousands of trait-associated loci across thousands of phenotypes \cite{abdellaoui_15_2023}, while rare disease studies have linked thousands of Mendelian conditions to specific causal genes \cite{chung_meta-analysis_2023}. A genetic association provides a gene with ``genetic support" for a human trait -- establishing the human-relevance and causal effect of its perturbation.

The number of trait-associated genes identified by genetic association studies is, however, but a small fraction of the total number of trait-relevant genes -- that is, those that can be perturbed in humans to yield an effect on a trait of interest\cite{finan_druggable_2017}. Many trait-relevant genes escape detection due to limited statistical power \cite{wang_statistical_2019}, constraining evolutionary pressure on genetic variation \cite{gazal_linkage_2017}, or the difficulty of assigning noncoding GWAS signals to their effector genes \cite{schipper_demystifying_2022,costanzo_realizing_2025}. Yet the full set of trait-relevant genes is of most interest for drug development and experimental study\cite{jhamb_pathway_2019,macnamara_network_2020}. In part due to this incompleteness, most translational research does not use human genetics to prioritize genes\cite{wehling_assessing_2009,dornbos_evaluating_2022}, even with the  increasing recognition that drug targets with genetic support are substantially more likely to succeed clinically\cite{plenge_validating_2013,minikel_refining_2024} 

% Related Work and More specific stage setting to elicit the the contributions paragraph
By contrast, non-human-genetic approaches are more widely used to identify trait-relevant genes but come with critical tradeoffs. Experimental studies in animal \cite{seok_genomic_2013,widjaja_inhibiting_2019} and cell-based \cite{ben-david_genetic_2018,canon_clinical_2019} systems can establish causal links between genes and phenotypes, but their relevance to humans is often uncertain. Human observational studies, such as gene expression profiling \cite{wu_integrating_2022,rodriguez_machine_2021} or epidemiological associations \cite{barter_cholesteryl_2011,bhatt_cardiovascular_2019}, provide human-relevant context but lack causal directionality. The literature and biomedical databases are rife with gene-trait relationships derived from these or other non-genetic studies, and they are widely used as a starting point for drug discovery or experimental study\cite{swinney_how_2011,dornbos_evaluating_2022}. Recent studies demonstrate how these sources can be rapidly and accurately queried with large language models (LLMs) \cite{toufiq_harnessing_2023,hu_evaluation_2025}. However, it is well documented that prioritizing genes using these evidence sources is prone to bias, incompleteness, or circularity\cite{brito_removing_2011,gillis_assessing_2013,gaudet_removing_2017,dunham_human_2018,haynes_gene_2018,stoeger_large_2018,richardson_meta_2024,tan_caution_2025}.

Ideally, a map of gene-trait relationships would combine the complementary benefits of human genetic and non-human-genetic evidence sources. It is commonplace to evaluate genes individually through manual expert synthesis of genetic data and the published literature\cite{mountjoy_open_2022,karjalainen_genome-wide_2024}, but a quantiative map across the genome and phenome requires scalabale statistical methods. Such methods have been developed both in the context of GWAS effector gene identification, where gene ``functional annotations'' enriched for genetic associations are used to prioritize genes nearby associated SNPs\cite{pers_biological_2015,weeks_leveraging_2023}, and in the context of rare disease diagnostics, where literature mining \cite{birgmeier_amelie_2020} and interaction networks\cite{smedley_next-generation_2015} are used to prioritize genes that carry variants observed in a patient. These approaches, however, rely on heuristics (such as harmonic averaging \cite{buniello_open_2025} or regressions using noisy or incomplete training data\cite{weeks_leveraging_2023,schipper_prioritizing_2025}) and often lack a biological model, which limits their portability across traits or application to settings other than that for which they were originally designed. As a result, the currently charted map of trait-gene relationships across rare and common diseases remains sparse, inaccurate, and inconsistent across traits.

% Contribution explanation
Here, we combine human genetic association data for rare and common traits and non-human-genetic data encoded in 600,000 gene annotations to construct a complete probabilistic evidence map relating 18,125 genes to 6,915 traits. Each gene-trait relationship is a composite of two statistically calibrated values: a traditional measure of  ``direct genetic support'' (the strength of gene-trait association from human genetic data) and a new  measure of ``indirect genetic support'' (the extent to which the gene shares functional annotations with other trait-associated genes). Indirect genetic support is measured on the same scale as direct genetic support, estimates a measure of gene-trait relevance distinct from direct gene-trait association, and enables us to use unbiased human genetic associations to calibrate noisy and heterogeneous non-human-genetic data sources and learn which are trustworthy for each trait. This map when thresholded is orders of magnitude denser than manually curated knowledge graphs and, when unthresholded, can be viewed as dual numerical embeddings of genes and traits learned from combined genetic and non-genetic data sources. We illustrate the utility of this resource to aid in the interpretation of gene function and trait mechanistic heterogeneity, predict drug target relative success more accurately and comprehensively than previous resources, and provide a resource for agentic AI not capturable by pretraining on text-only corpora.

To map gene-trait relationships across the entire phenome and genome, we sought to infer a probability of relevance for any gene  to any trait. Doing so using available human genetic associations and non-human-genetic data posed several challenges. First, we needed to infer relationships across thousands of traits accurately, robustly, and efficiently. Second, the traits are heterogeneous in terms of genetic association data available (e.g. common variant associations for GWAS, Mendelian gene-disease validity assertions for rare diseases) and genetic architecture. Third, currently significant gene-trait associations cover only a small fraction of trait-relevant genes. Fourth, non-human-genetic datasets (i.e. curated pathway databases, results from animal model experiments, text-mined relationships, or gene expression enrichment analyses) are large in number and of varying quality, and it is unclear which datasets are useful for predicting trait-relevant genes. Finally, non-human-genetic datasets are biased toward well-studied genes and well-studied biological mechanisms.

To address these challenges, we developed a statistical model of a quantity we term a gene's ``indirect genetic support'', and a method termed PIGEAN (Priors Inferred from GEne ANnotations) for homogeneously inferring across common and rare traits. Indirect genetic support combines the unbiased rigor of genetic associations with the breadth and scope of gene annotations, which encode non-genetic-data either as gene sets (binary annotations) or gene quantitative vectors (continuous annotations) and can be constructed in a high-throughput manner from many biomedical data sources (\ref{sec:methods}). Indirect genetic support is an emergent property of a simple statistical model of observed genetic associations, in which gene annotations affect the probability that a gene is relevant to a trait, with effect sizes unknown and different for each annotation and each trait, and in which trait-relevant genes are more likely to produce genetic associations than are non-trait-relevant genes (\ref{sec:methods}).  This model statistically generalizes several widely used heuristic approaches for prioritizing genes from genetic association studies (\ref{sec:methods}), and it enables us to demonstrate mathematically that genetic associations not only directly implicate the genes for which they are observed but also indirectly implicate genes with shared annotations. PIGEAN applies Bayesian inference to this model to jointly infer gene annotation effect sizes and gene trait-relevance probabilities given a library of gene annotations and either GWAS summary statistics or rare disease gene lists, in so doing (a) using human genetic associations to select annotations with non-zero effects and calibrate their effect sizes and (b) using genetic annotations to identify additional trait-relevant genes beyond those with direct association evidence. Priors on the latent parameters enforce strict regularization that control biases towards well-studied genes or profligate annotations, as well as enforcing model sparsity and interpretability (\ref{sec:methods}).

We studied PIGEAN across a universe of 5,030 complex traits and 1,526 rare disease groups. For complex traits, we integrated full summary statistic files from XXX GWAS covering XXX traits, as well as curated association lists from XXX GWAS across XXX traits. We used an extension of a previously published approach \cite{dornbos_evaluating_2022} to calculate each gene's direct genetic support from variant-level associations (\ref{sec:methods}).  For rare diseases, we mapped curated disease–gene confidence levels from Orphanet to probabilities of disease relevance (\ref{sec:methods}).  

To evaluate the contribution of non-genetic evidence, we constructed multiple PIGEAN models, each defined by a specific set of geneset libraries available during inference. These libraries collectively included 144,627 gene annotations spanning diverse sources (\ref{Supplementary Table 2}): knockout mouse phenotypes (11,502 Mammalian Phenotype Ontology terms), membership in pathways and curated gene sets (29,003 from the Molecular Signatures Database), co-occurrence in PubMedCentral manuscript figures (19,391 sets from the Pathway Figure OCR project), co-occurrence with terms in PubMed abstracts (25,126 MeSH terms), network interaction partners (37,156 STRING neighbors), and single-cell gene expression patterns (41,840 experimental clusters curated as part of the PoPS method).  

The resulting map (Figure \ref{fig:full_map}), generated from the top-performing model identified via model selection (See \textit{Validation of Resource}), produced a complete numerical matrix of gene–trait relevance probabilities. This included 135,188 gene-trait relationships (21 per trait and 7 per gene) with a posterior probability (PP) above 90\% (i.e. false discovery rate < 0.1), 295,547 gene–trait relationships (45 per trait and 16 per gene) with PP>50\%, and 10.49M significant (\ref{sec:methods}) gene set–trait associations. Each gene-trait relationship is supported by provenance that includes the specific SNP associations or curated gene records  that produce direct support, and the specific gene set-trait relationsips that produce indirect support. The full map is available for download, for interactive browsing via a web interface, or through AI-driven query to filter and explore results in response to high-level questions.

For common traits, many of these result when genes nearby GWAS hits are identified based on their strong indirect support relative to neighboring genes. We next sought to systematically validate PIGEAN along two goals: (i) predicting genes that are biologically and clinically meaningful and (ii) identifying gene sets that are mechanistically aligned with each phenotype. To do so, we evaluated a focused benchmark comprising $n=13$ common traits selected for representative clinical coverage and statistical power, and $n=40$ rare traits partitioned into $n=20$ disease-specific lists and $n=20$ disorder-category lists (see Methods). Because our full universe spans $>7,000$ traits, an exhaustive search over the model space was computationally infeasible; instead, we executed a targeted model-selection procedure that iteratively pruned candidates while answering specific research questions about PIGEAN’s behavior. For model selection, we defined a Minimum Description Length (MDL) criterion \cite{} that balances complexity and accuracy, where complexity captures both the number of gene sets made available to the model and the number of gene sets deemed significant for prediction, and accuracy integrates (a) potential clinical success of nominated targets and (b) mechanistic alignment between significant gene-set semantics and the phenotype under study. Operationally, this yielded three independent axes for comparison across models: (1) clinical success, quantified as the relative success \cite{minikel_refining_2024} of PIGEAN-prioritized genes that exceed a threshold of direct/indirect genetic support; (2) mechanistic alignment, quantified as the normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG) of semantic similarity ranks between significant gene-set names and their corresponding phenotype; and (3) parsimony, quantified as the balance between the number of predictive gene sets surpassing a $\beta$ threshold and the total size of the gene-set libraries included.

Our final validation step was designed to assess the potential impact of circularity between GWAS results and curated geneset libraries in predicting drug targets as well as in forecasting future GWAS significance. Because many published genesets are curated from GWAS signals, we sought to confirm that PIGEAN was not simply reinforcing known signals but was instead capable of highlighting novel associations. Similarly, we repeated our drug target relative success analysis using only targets that would not have been available prior to the publication of a given geneset. To this end, we created 10 additional models using gene set sources from MsigDB, Mouse Phenotypes, and MeSH Terms. For each library, we curated pre-2018 and post-2018 versions based on provenance information. We then applied our validation analyses across 67 traits with both pre- and post-2018 GWAS. For the relative success analyses, only drug targets launched after 2018 were included to avoid target circularity.  

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:validation-figure-2}a-b, we observe consistent improvements in relative success as a function of both direct and indirect support, even after temporally excluding any form of GWAS or drug target information. Notably, the pre-2018 text-mining model, which explicitly removed all GWAS and drug MeSH terms (Methods), achieved the strongest performance. At a direct genetic support log-odds threshold of 4 ($P=98.2\%$ relevance), this model reached a relative success of 4.51 (95\% CI: 2.67–7.63) across 50 predicted targets, significantly above the Magma baseline ($p$-value $=2.5\times 10^{-6}$). Although somewhat weaker, indirect genetic support also improved relative success, reaching 3.04 (95\% CI: 1.93–4.80) at the same log-odds threshold with 108 predicted targets. This highlights an important distinction between Figure~\ref{fig:validation-figure-2}a and~\ref{fig:validation-figure-2}b: indirect support identifies additional targets not captured by direct genetic support alone, as reflected in the plotted trend lines showing the number of predicted targets as a function of the (in)direct support thresholds. We further analyze the complementarity of direct and indirect support in expanding drug target diversity in the following section.  

We next evaluated how well direct and indirect genetic support could predict future GWAS significance by regressing on post-2018 MAGMA Z-scores. When directly regressing Z-scores (Methods), we observed modest but statistically significant gains in model fit ($\Delta R^2$) when incorporating indirect support, with maximum improvements across all traits of rangeing from 0.0019 to 0.017 (Extended Data Figure XXXX). The proportion of traits with significant $\Delta R^2$ also varied by model (ranging from 62.9\% to 91.4\%). However, substantial noise remained in non-significant genes, where MAGMA Z-scores were nearly random, such that even baseline regressions of pre-2018 versus post-2018 Z-scores yielded a low average $R^2 = 0.14$ across 67 traits.  

To test whether genetic support could act as a filter to reduce noise and forcast genes that will be predictive of future GWAS, we regressed post-2018 MAGMA Z-scores on pre-2018 scores while progressively restricting to genes meeting increasing thresholds of direct or indirect genetic support (Figure~\ref{fig:validation-figure-2}c-d). Across all models, regression slopes remained positive for both direct ($\beta$ range: 0.014–0.038) and indirect ($\beta$ range: 0.050–0.058) support, with indirect support providing a greater increase in model fit. Importantly, filtering on stronger evidence not only increased average $\Delta R^2$ but also strengthened the concordance of pre- and post-2018 associations, consistent with the notion that PIGEAN prioritizes genes with reproducible relevance. Together, these temporal validations support that PIGEAN is not simply recapitulating latent GWAS or drug target information embedded in curated libraries, but instead enriches for signals that are predictive of future discoveries.

% Introduce PIGEAN and how it instantiates the concept of indiret genetic support efficiently
We define indirect genetic support as the probabilistic influence that genetically implicated genes exert on related genes through shared (functional) annotations. These annotations, such as gene sets from , provide structured, non-genetic information that links genes based on common biological contexts (Fig.~\ref{fig:pigean-concept}). While indirect support could in principle be modeled in various ways, our approach formalizes it as an informed prior (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}) that intends to capture more prior relevance if a gene is in one or multiple shared contexts that have other genes with high direct genetic support for a trait. For each gene set library, we construct a matrix, $X$, that encodes gene–annotation relationships, and estimate the contribution of each annotation to trait relevance, $\beta$, using a sparsity-enforced logistic regression model (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}). We enforce sparsity by sampling the hyper-prior distribution over $\beta$ as a spike-and-slab distribution \cite{mitchell_bayesian_1988,madigan_model_1994,ishwaran_spike_2005} (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}, Extended Data Fig~\ref{}). We find that biologically related traits tend to prioritize similar gene sets, supporting the model’s ability to recover coherent and interpretable biological structure (Fig.~\ref{fig:geneset-cluster}).

% Explain direct support calculation
We employ multiple methods for modeling direct genetic support based on the type of genetic data available for a phenotype. If GWAS is available, we use an extension of a previously proposed Bayesian model \cite{dornbos_evaluating_2022} that retains simplicity and speed but includes extensions to account for suggestive association signals, the number of genes nearby a SNP association, association fine mapping, and probabilistic SNP to gene linkage functions (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}). For rare disease associations, we use manually annotated probabilities of association derived from public databases. Additional extensions to complex trait exome sequence associations or rare disease de novo or case control Bayesian models \cite{TADA} are straightforward (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}) but full analysis of these generatlizations are left to future work.

% Explain the model parameters and fitting using the iterative gibbs sampling
For each trait, we jointly fit this model across all genes and all gene sets through Gibbs sampling. In essence, this approach alternates between updating the gene genetic support estimates and the annotation effect estiamtes (\hyperlink{pigeanpior}{Methods}); in this way, genetic support estimates are refined as information passes through annotations shared by genes. It is this perspective that motivates the definition of ``indirect genetic support" (the contribution to genetic support from gene annotations and their effects) as an extension to the traditional notion of direct genetic support (the contribution from the genetic data for a gene).  At convergence the Gibbs sampler yields (a) jointly estimated effects on indirect support across all annotations in the model, and (b) combined genetic support values for all genes in the genome. To dissect these results, we also record (c) the marginal effects on indirect support from each annotation (i.e. not controlling for other annotations in the model, a value analogous to the traditional notion of gene set enrichment \cite{?}) and (d) a decomposition of the combined genetic support for each gene into direct and indirect values. The full model is depicted as a probabilistic graphical model in Fig.~\ref{fig:pigean-concept}C.

Specifically, given an initial estimate of gene relevance based on direct genetic support, we infer which gene sets are most informative by learning their contribution to explaining these relevance scores (logistic regression). We then use these refined gene set weights to update our estimates of gene relevance, now informed by both genetic and annotation contexts. By iterating these two steps by refining annotation weights and updating gene relevance, we converge on a joint distribution that captures both direct genetic signals and their propagation through structured biological knowledge. This inference procedure is implemented efficiently via Gibbs sampling, and the full model is depicted as a probabilistic graphical model in Fig.~\ref{fig:pigean-concept}.

A common concern in the genetics community is the potential \emph{circularity} introduced when incorporating curated gene annotations (e.g., pathways, enrichments) alongside genetic data (e.g., GWAS, WES) in statistical models. This concern stems from the fact that both sources may reflect overlapping biological signals. Moreover, many gene annotations may themselves have been constructed with knowledge of prior genetic findings, such as GWAS hits, and potentially resulting in a double-counting effect when used together in gene prioritization models and confounding downstream analyses.

However, as illustrated by our proposed causal diagram (Figure~\ref{fig:pigean-causal-model}a), this circularity is not intrinsic if the underlying causal relationships are correctly specified. In particular, if both annotations ($X$) and genetic data ($S$) are treated as noisy observations of an unobserved biological state ($U$), and we jointly condition on both in a properly structured model, then the use of both sources does not induce circularity, even if gene annotations were partially informed by genetic data. 

We demonstrate this in a toy example (Figure~\ref{fig:pigean-causal-model}b) using simulated structural equations (see Methods) where the task is to recover the ground truth relevance vector $d \in \{0,1\}$ across an arbitrary set of genes $g_1, \dots, g_N$. Even as the correlation between $S$ and $X$ increases, simulating stronger causal influence of genetics on annotations, our model’s predictive performance (AUC) remains stable. The only observed effect is an inflation in the standard error of the effect size associated with $S$, which impacts classical p-value calculation but not the Bayesian posterior. In a Bayesian framework, this uncertainty is naturally integrated into inference, mitigating its impact.

Our method, PIGEAN (\textbf{P}riors \textbf{I}nferred from \textbf{GE}ne \textbf{AN}notations) implements a joint Bayesian model aligned with the causal structure in Figure~\ref{fig:pigean-causal-model}a. For a trait $T$ and genes $g_1, \dots, g_N$, let:
\begin{itemize}
\item $d_i \in \{0,1\}$ denote whether gene $g_i$ is causally relevant to the trait,
\item $\mathbf{X}_i = \{X_{ij}\}$ denote the set of annotations for gene $g_i$ (e.g., gene set memberships, pathway inclusion, expression scores),
\item $S_i$ denote the genetic evidence for $g_i$ (e.g., effect size, summary p-value).
\end{itemize}

The key innovation in PIGEAN is its use of $\mathbf{X}_i$ and learned weights $\beta$ to define an informative prior over $d_i$ via a logistic link:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:priors}
\log\frac{y_i}{1-y_i} = \sum_j X_{ij}\beta_j + \alpha_i + \log\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}
\end{equation}
\begin{align*}
d_i \sim \text{Bern}(y_i) \quad
\beta_j \sim \delta_j\mathbf{N}(0, \sigma_j^2) \quad
\alpha_i = \alpha\sum_j X_{ij} \quad
\sigma_j^2 = \sigma^2\text{Var}_i(X_{ij})^w \quad
\delta_j \sim \text{Bern}(p) \quad
\end{align*}

This construction allows us to model $d_i$ using a ``principled prior" derived from functional annotations, mirroring how domain experts reason about gene relevance using prior biological knowledge. While this prior depends on $X_i$ and $\beta$, it does not violate our causal model: in Bayesian inference, a misspecified prior can be corrected by the likelihood, and uncertainty in the prior is naturally incorporated into the posterior.

We then incorporate the genetic score $S_i$ as independent, complementary evidence for $d_i$. Since $S_i$ is a child of $d_i$ in the causal graph, we can model $P(S_i \mid d_i)$ (see Methods). The posterior distribution over $d$ and $\beta$ factors naturally via conditional independence:

\begin{equation}
\underbrace{P(d, \beta \mid S, X)}_{\text{joint posterior}} \propto 
\underbrace{P(S \mid d)}_{\text{direct evidence (likelihood)}} \cdot 
\underbrace{P(d \mid X, \beta)}_{\text{indirect evidence (prior)}} \cdot 
\underbrace{P(\beta)}_{\text{hyperprior}}
\end{equation}

Note that $S_i$ is conditionally independent of $(\mathbf{X}_i, \beta)$ given $d_i$, and $X_i$ is conditionally independent of $d_i$ given $U_i$ (which is latent). While marginalizing over $U_i$ induces a dependency between $X_i$ and $d_i$, we model this relationship explicitly via the logistic prior in Equation~\ref{eq:priors}. Moreover, $\beta$ does not enter the likelihood $P(S_i \mid d_i)$ and plays no role in modeling the direct genetic evidence.

A full visual overview of the PIGEAN workflow is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pigean-concept}. A complete model specification and inference details are provided in the Methods section.

\subsection{Validation on Simulations} \label{sec:validation-sim}
We validated PIGEAN’s behavior through a suite of simulations designed to assess both specificity and robustness under varying conditions. As expected, when applied to null GWAS datasets, PIGEAN correctly inferred annotation effect sizes near zero and returned negligible direct evidence, demonstrating no spurious signal inflation. This was also true when annotations were randomly permuted across genes, supporting the specificity of the model’s inference procedure. These results suggest that combined gene relevance incorporating both direct and indirect evidence and measured as an approximate Bayes Factor (ABF) greater than 3 (measured in log odds) and annotation effect sizes above 0.005 can serve as conservative thresholds for study-wide significance (Extended Data Figure~\ref{fig:null_sim}, Supplementary Table 3).

Further simulations highlighted the distinct effects of annotation noise and genetic association noise on model performance. Focusing on annotation noise, we observed four key properties: (1) PIGEAN conservatively estimates annotation effects and gene ABFs due to spike-and-slab shrinkage; (2) annotations with negative effects or weak evidence are more aggressively shrunk, particularly in settings with few disease-relevant genes; (3) accuracy improves with increased annotation effect size and sparsity level; and (4) genes with many annotations must be regularized to avoid ABF inflation from asymmetric standard errors. Importantly, model performance was robust to hyperparameter mis-specification, with even large deviations in $\sigma$ and $p$ (specified in Equation~\ref{eq:priors}) yielding stable inference accuracy. Simulations incorporating GWAS summary statistics further confirmed that while association noise can reduce power, it does not induce false positives. PIGEAN’s conservative estimates under both annotation and association noise affirm its reliability for gene prioritization tasks. For more information, see Methods.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{PIGEAN Model}
\hypertarget{pigeanpior}{The} PIGEAN model specifies that (a) a subset of genes are ``relevant" to a trait and (b) the probability a gene is relevant to a trait is affected (additively) by the gene’s annotations. Specifically, for an arbitrary trait $T$ and genes $g_i\ldots g_N$, let $d_i \in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether gene $i$ is involved in $T$ and let $y_i=\Pr(d_i=1)$. Given a set of (binary or real valued) annotations $X_{ij}$ (where $j=1\dots M$) for $g_i$, we then model:
\[
\log\frac{y_i}{1-y_i} = \sum_j X_{ij}\beta_j + \alpha_i + \log\frac{\pi}{1-\pi} 
\]
\begin{align*}
\beta_j \sim \delta_j\mathbf{N}(0, \sigma_j^2) \quad
\alpha_i = \alpha\sum_j X_{ij} \quad
\sigma_j^2 = \sigma^2\text{Var}_i(X_{ij})^w \quad
\delta_j \sim \text{Bern}(p) \quad
\end{align*}
where $\pi$ is the prior probability that a gene is involved in disease, which we typically set to 0.05 ($\sim 1000$ genes) for complex traits and 0.01 ($\sim 200$ genes) for rare diseases, and the four parameters ($\alpha$, $\sigma$, $w$, and $p$) enforce regularization: $\sigma$ and $w$ penalize high variance (i.e. “large”) genes and annotations, $\sigma$ shrinks gene annotation effect sizes, and $p$ enforces sparsity of gene annotation effect sizes. As described in the Online Methods, we learn $p$ from the input data for trait $T$, set $w$ and $\alpha$ to limit dependence of estimands on the variance of annotations or genes, and set $\sigma$ to an empirically determined large trait-independent value (learning trait-specific $\sigma$ values is possible but, empirically, has little impact on results and introduces the potential for less robustness during model application across many traits).

We do not know $d_i$ or $\beta_j$ (they are latent variables), but we can jointly infer them conditional upon observed genetic association statistics $\mathcal{S}$. By employing Gibbs sampling, we can sample from this distribution $\prob(d, \beta \mid \mathcal{S})$ if we can sample from $\prob(d \mid \beta, \mathcal{S})$ and $\prob(\beta \mid d, \mathcal{S})=\prob(\beta \mid d)$. We can apply previous ideas[] to sample from $\prob(\beta \mid d)$ (Methods), and we consider four types of genetic association statistics of increasing complexity for calculating the conditional probability $\prob(d \mid \beta, \mathcal{S})$: rare diseases in which a subset of disease-relevant genes are "known", WES gene-level burden test statistics, genome-wide SNP summary statistics from a GWAS, and a list of only significant SNPs from a GWAS. More complicated scenarios including multiple types of summary statistics (e.g. GWAS summary statistics alongside WES burden tests of ultra-rare variants) can be handled in a straightforward manner, provided the summary statistics are independent (see Methods).

In the rare disease scenario, we assume that we are given $\prob(d \mid \mathcal{S})$, either fixed to a high value for a small number of "known" genes or estimated from qualitative confidence "tiers"[].  It is straightforward (Methods) to show that the log of the conditional odds of $d_i$ is the sum of its prior odds conditional upon $\beta$ and the unconditional gene bayes factor (which can be calculated from $\prob(d \mid \mathcal{S})$ and $\pi$), under the assumption that gene annotations included in the model are not influenced by knowledge of disease genes for $T$ (we investigate the feasibility of this assumption and the consequences of its violation below). In the WES scenario, the calculations are identical except, rather than being given $\prob(d \mid \mathcal{S})$ and converting it to gene bayes factors, we estimate the gene bayes factors from the burden effect sizes and standard errors using an approach previously developed for SNPs (Methods).

In the genome-wide GWAS summary statistics scenario, we calculate $\prob(d \mid \beta, \mathcal{S})$ using an extension of the HuGE calculator, a previously proposed set of simple rules to estimate gene-level probabilities of disease relevance conditional upon nearby SNP associations (Methods). The extensions we use within PIGEAN (referred to as HuGEX; see Methods) address some of the obvious limitations of the HuGE calculator while preserving its advantages (simplicity, speed, and interpretability). In the  scenario of partial GWAS summary statistics, we apply the HuGEX model to all genes nearby SNPs with summary statistics available, and set the remaining genes to have $\prob(d \mid \beta, \mathcal{S})=\prob(d \mid \beta)$. 

The joint posterior distribution $\Pr(d,\beta|\mathcal{S})$ inferred by PIGEAN can be distilled into four sets of summary statistics (Methods): $E[\beta_j]$, the effect size for the $j^{th}$  gene annotation; $ABF^I_i$, the “indirect” approximate Bayes Factor (ABF) that represents evidence from the annotations for gene $g_i$; $ABF^D_i$, the “direct” ABF that represents evidence from genetic associations for gene $g_i$; and $ABF_i$, the combined $ABF$ for gene $g_i$. The overall probability of disease relevance for a gene can then be expressed straightforwardly as a function of $ABF_i$ and the prior $\pi$. 

These equations offer three interpretations of the PIGEAN model (Methods). In the "prinicpled priors" interpretation, the posterior probability of a gene's disease relevance is estimated using genetic associations as data ($ABF_i^D$) and the annotations to adjust the prior (a combination of $ABF_i^I$ and $\pi$), offering bayesian approach to use both genetic associations and gene annotations to prioritize genes. In the "bottom-up" interpretation, PIGEAN generalizes the popular PoPS approach; it can be shown (Methods) that if MAGMA Z-scores are used to estimate the log-odds of $ABF_i^D$, sparsity constraints on the $\beta_j$ values are replaced by L2 regularization, and full Gibbs sampling from the posterior is replaced by a single regression, then PIGEAN reduces to PoPS --- these alterations elucidate the quantity PoPS  estimates as well as the approximations in inference and data that it makes. Finally, the "indirect genetic support" interpretation of PIGEAN demonstrates that genetic support is due not only to genetic associations nearby a gene ($ABF_i^D$) but also to genetic associations nearby other genes ($ABF_i^I$) that indirectly support the gene through shared annotations.

We implemented PIGEAN as a Python program and applied it to XXX full summary statistic GWAS datasets from the Association to Function Knowledge Portal, lists of reported significant GWAS associations for X traits from the GWAS catalog, X WES burden statistic datasets from XXX, and XXX rare disease gene lists from Orphanet (\textbf{Supplementary Table 1}). For validation purposes, we also analyzed 20 simulated “null” GWAS in which all SNPs had zero true effect sizes, and 10 simulated GWAS in which SNPs had true effect sizes consistent with the PIGEAN model (Methods). We considered a total of 144,627 gene annotations, including (Supplementary Table 2) knockout mouse phenotypes (11,502 Mammalian Phenotype Ontology terms), membership in gene sets and pathways (29,003 gene sets from the Molecular Signatures Database), occurrence in a PubMedCentral manuscript figure (19,391 gene sets from the Pathway Figure OCR project), co-occurrence with terms in PubMed abstracts (25,126 MeSH terms), network interaction partners (37,156 gene neighbors from STRING), and single-cell gene expression patterns (41,840 experiment clusters curated as part of the PoPS method).

\subsection{Validation on Simulations}
As a negative control, we first applied PIGEAN to the 20 “null” simulated GWAS and found that, as expected, inferred gene annotation effect sizes were all close to zero (Extended Data Figure~\ref{fig:null_sim}a) as were indirect, direct, and combined gene ABFs (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:null_sim}bcd). Similarly, when we applied PIGEAN to the 50 real GWAS but randomly permuted genes within the 166,868 gene annotations, we also observed annotation effect sizes close to zero (Supplementary  Figure \ref{fig:null_sim}e). Across our 20 null simulations and 166,868 annotations only one gene had combined gene ABF above 3 (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:null_sim}g), with the dominant contribution coming from direct ABFs (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:null_sim}h), and across the 50 real GWAS with permuted annotations, only one annotation had effect size above 0.004 (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:null_sim}f). Based on these results, annotation effect sizes of 0.005 and gene combined ABFs of 3 roughly correspond to “study-wide significant” p-value thresholds (Supplementary Table 3).

We also used simulations with non-zero annotation effect sizes to understand the behavior of PIGEAN in response to different model parameters. Accuracy of PIGEAN is affected by noise from two sources: the incomplete ability of annotations to predict disease-relevant genes (“annotation noise”), and the incomplete ability of summary statistics to identify disease-relevant genes (“association noise”). Focusing first on annotation noise, we ran PIGEAN with a truncated model in which we treated each gene’s probability of disease relevance ($d_i$) as observed rather than inferred from summary statistics. These simulations yielded four main findings. First, PIGEAN in general conservatively estimates both annotation effect sizes (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:non_null_sim}a) and gene ABFs (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:non_null_sim}b), due to its “spike and slab” regularization that sets estimated effect sizes to zero and shrinks estimated effect sizes toward zero. Second, annotations with negative effects (i.e. that reduce genetic support) were shrunk substantially more than those with positive effects, as (consequently) were genes with ABFs < 1, an asymmetry that naturally results from the higher standard errors for negative gene set effect sizes when there are only a small number of disease-relevant genes (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:non_null_sim}cd). Third, PIGEAN’s accuracy was also affected by simulation parameters, with more accurate inferences possible for larger simulated effect size magnitudes (larger $\sigma$ Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_params}a) and (to a lesser extent) more non-zero simulated effect sizes (larger $p$; Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_params}b). Additional noise added to the simulated gene ABFs affected accuracy of the inferred ABFs but (perhaps non-intuitively) did not affect accuracy of the inferred annotation effect sizes (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_params}c). Finally, due to the nature of Gibbs sampling, estimates produced by PIGEAN varied across runs, but with sufficient numbers of iterations and chains estimates were stable (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_stability}).

These simulations confirmed the importance PIGEAN’s joint inference and regularization features. First, its full joint inference procedure (in which gene and gene set values are iteratively re-estimated) increased accuracy compared to a model in which gene set effect sizes were only estimated once (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_onestep}). Second, even though simulated gene ABFs had no dependence on the number of annotations for the gene, regularization (via its  parameter) to penalize genes with many annotations was necessary to avoid biases and ABF accuracy reductions induced (as described in Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:non_null_sim}cd) by asymmetric annotation standard errors (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_induced}). Similarly, even though simulated annotation effect sizes had no dependence on the number of genes with the annotation, regularization (via its w parameter) to penalize “large” annotations was necessary to avoid biases induced by the smaller standard errors of larger annotations (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_reg}; Methods).

For simulations with a small number of non-zero annotation effects, PIGEAN was able to infer reasonably accurate estimates of its sparsity parameter ($p$) (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_param_infer}), although it significantly underestimated $p$ as the fraction of non-zero effects increased. Our attempts to estimate PIGEAN’s shrinkage parameter ($\sigma$) for individual simulations suggested that there are typically too few annotations  to estimate this parameter accurately, and we therefore tuned this parameter empirically (Methods). Fortunately, varying these parameters from their simulated values by even an order of magnitude did not dramatically reduce PIGEAN’s estimation accuracy (Supplementary Figures \ref{fig:sim_vary_sigma} and \ref{fig:sim_vary_p}).

In addition to these simulations focused exclusively on “annotation noise”, we also evaluated the impact of  “association noise” on PIGEAN’s performance – that is, the reality that (due to finite sample sizes and the vagaries of evolution) many disease-relevant genes will not lie near detectable association signals. We studied association noise in the context of GWAS summary statistics, given the well-documented difficulties in inferring effector genes from these data. For each of three annotation noise settings (Methods), we simulated five GWAS of increasing sample sizes (using linkage disequilibrium from the 1000 Genomes Project, SNP to gene linkages from a previously published method, and SNP effect size parameters from previously published simulations; Methods). As expected, the use of GWAS data rather than observed disease probabilities degraded the accuracy of PIGEAN (Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}a). With perfect knowledge of SNP to gene linkages Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}b), less annotation noise Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}c), or larger sample sizes Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}d), the accuracy of PIGEAN increased, but only to limited extent. Accuracy was much higher for marginal annotation effect sizes than for joint effect sizes Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}e), which is a common feature of multivariate models with correlated predictors. When PIGEAN inferred its regularization parameters, rather than being given the true parameters used in simulations, accuracy degraded further Supplementary Figure \ref{fig:sim_gwas_gene_set}f). Notably, however, in all scenarios PIGEAN conservatively estimated annotation effect sizes, suggesting that the overwhelming impact of GWAS annotation noise is to decrease power rather than to increase false positives.

We conclude that, under idealized simulations, PIGEAN behaves broadly as expected. Three noteworthy properties are (a) the difficulty of inferring negative genetic support (due to known properties of GWAS but perhaps unappreciated properties of models with few disease-relevant genes); (b) the importance of regularization and iterative inference in gene set analysis, and (c) it conservative nature when confronted with annotation noise and and association noise.

\section{Methods}

Our strategy to infer mechanisms underlying GWAS associations for a trait has two steps (Figure 1). In the first step, we use PIGEAN to identify genes with genetic support, and gene sets that impact genetic support, from the GWAS summary statistics (p-values and effect sizes for each SNP). In the second step, we assemble a (sparse) matrix of genes with high genetic support and gene sets with large effects on genetic support (with entries indicating which genes are within each set), we run bayesian non-negative matrix factorization with automatic relevance determination to identify latent factors each characterized by sparse and positive loadings for each gene and each gene set, and we use the gene sets and their loadings to label each factor using a large language model (LLM). The interpretation of these results is collectively (a) estimates of genetic support for the trait for each gene, separated into direct (significant associations linked to the gene) and indirect (disease-relevant annotations of the gene) genetic support estimates; (b) a list of gene sets that affect indirect genetic support, with effects characterized both by marginal values (when the gene set is considered in isolation as in existing gene set enrichment approaches) and joint values (when all gene sets are considered together); and (c) a list of latent factors that simultaneously and quantitatively group genes and gene sets into higher order mechanisms. The latent factors are designed to capture higher order biological mechanisms for which existing gene set libraries are noisy, biased, and incomplete proxies.

There are three novel components to this approach. The first novel component is the PIGEAN model, which probabilistically generalizes the widely used PoPs approach for effector gene prioritization.   assumes that a subset of genes are involved in each trait and that the probability a gene is involved in a trait is affected (additively) by the gene’s annotations. Specifically for an arbitrary trait $T$, genes $i=1, \dots, N$, and annotations $j=1, \dots, M$, let $d_i$ indicate whether gene $i$ is involved in $T$, let $j$ indicate the degree to which annotation $j$ affects this probability, and let $X_{ij}$ indicate the value of annotation $j$ for gene $i$. We first model the log-odds of the probability di as a weighted sum of the annotations for gene $i$,

\begin{align*}
\prob(z_{ik} \mid d_i) &= f(d_i) \\
d_i &\sim \text{Bern}(y_i) \\
\log\frac{y_i}{1-y_i} &= \sum_j X_{ij}\beta_j + \alpha_i + \log\frac{\pi}{1-\pi} \\
\beta_j &\sim \delta_j\mathbf{N}(0, \sigma_j^2) \\
\alpha_i &= \alpha\sum_j X_{ij} \\
\sigma_j^2 &= \sigma^2\text{Var}_i(X_{ij})^w \\
\delta_j &\sim \text{Bern}(p) \\
\end{align*}

Each node $\beta_j$ corresponds to a gene set $j$, and
\begin{equation*}
    X_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
1 & g_i \in \text{gene set } j \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

where $\pi$ the prior probability that a gene is involved in disease (we typically set this to 0.05, corresponding to roughly 1000 genes, for complex traits) and the four parameters ($\alpha$, $\sigma$ , $w$, and $p$) enforce regularization: $\sigma$ and $w$ penalize high variance (i.e. “large”) genes and annotations,  shrinks gene annotation effect sizes, and $p$ enforces sparsity of gene annotation effect sizes. We learn values for $\sigma$ and $p$ from the input data (Methods) and set $w$ and $\alpha$ to enforce independence between the variance of annotations or genes and estimands.

A third interpretation is as a statistical generalization of the popular PoPS approach, with (roughly) a dichotomous model of gene disease relevance in place of MAGMA Z-scores, sparsity constraints on the $j$ values in place of L2 regularization, and full Gibbs sampling that enables iterative re-estimation of both gene and gene set values in place of a single regression and cross-validation.

$\sigma$ is hard to learn with a small number of gene sets. Therefore, we set it to a top value of 20\% for mouse genes (from Flannick et all) and assume 1\% of them are causal. This is supported by learning p across many traits (see Methods). Future work could estimate sigma/sigma\_power from data, as the sigma power does depend on the $\sigma$ (Figure)

We do not know which genes are involved in each trait, nor do we know the degree to which each annotation affects this probability ($d_i$ and $\beta_j$ are unobserved). However, if we further assume that observed association strength between a trait and a variant is affected by whether or not the variant is linked to a gene is involved in the trait, we can jointly infer $d_i$ and $beta_j$ conditional upon observed variant association statistics $z_k$ – that is, $\prob(d_i, \beta_j \mid z_k)$. By employing Gibbs sampling, we can sample from this distribution if we analytically solve for $\prob(d_i \mid \beta_j, z_k)$ and $\prob(\beta_j \mid d_i, z_k)=\prob(\beta_j \mid d_i)$. We can apply previous ideas to calculate $\prob(\beta_j \mid d_i)$ (Methods), while the model for $\prob(d_i\mid \beta_j, z_k)$ is more complex. Ideally, this model would incorporate (a) evidence linking variants to genes; (b) the $z_k$ values for variants linked to a gene; (c) variant annotations (e.g. bioinformatic or epigenomic); and (d) LD relationships among variants. For the current version of PIGEAN, we use a simpler model for $\prob(d_i \mid \beta_j, z_k)$ that builds upon previously proposed Human Genetic Evidence (HuGE) scores for calculating genetic support from common and rare variant associations[Dornbos]. We incorporate (see Methods) several extensions to the previously common variant HuGE score model that address some of its obvious limitations while preserving its advantages (namely, simplicity, speed, and interpretability).\\

First, its model is analogous to that used in models of polygenic SNP effects, with gene annotations playing the role of SNPs, genes playing the role of individuals, and a binary/logistic model used in place of a continuous model for the gene “phenotype”; in fact, the method used for sampling from $(\beta_j \mid d_i,z_k)$ is identical to that used by the LD-pred algorithm for sampling from the joint distribution of SNP effects. A second interpretation of PIGEAN is as a method to calculate the implication of assumptions about causal genes given observed SNP associations and “prior” knowledge about the gene’s function; in this framing, the posterior odds of a gene’s disease-relevance can be expressed in a Bayesian framework as 
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\prob(d_i \mid \beta_j, z_k)}{\prob(\neg d_i \mid \beta_j,z_k)} =
    \frac{\prob(z_k\mid d_i)}{\prob(z_k \mid \neg d_i)} \cdot \frac{\prob(d_i \mid \beta_j)}{\prob(\neg d_i \mid \beta_j)}
\end{equation}
or an update of the gene’s prior (given its annotations) after observing data (the nearby SNP associations). Finally, PIGEAN can be viewed as calculating genetic support for a gene that statistically integrates both “direct” support from nearby variant associations and “indirect” support from variant associations nearby other genes; these associations nearby other genes can indirectly support the gene if the other genes share annotations with the gene.

Given an input GWAS and collection of gene annotations, PIGEAN infers posterior distributions over $d_i$ for all genes and $\beta_j$ for all gene annotations. We distill these into four sets of summary statistics (Methods): $E[\beta_j]$, the effect size for each gene annotation $\beta_j$; $ABF^I_i$, the “indirect” approximate Bayes Factor (ABF) that represents evidence from the annotations for gene $i$; $ABF^D_i$, the “direct” ABF that represents evidence from genetic associations for gene i; and ABFi, the combined $ABF$ for gene $i$. Approximately, 
\begin{align*}
    \frac{\prob(d_i\mid \beta_j, z_k)}{\prob(\neg d_i \mid \beta_j, z_k)} \approx \frac{\pi}{1-\pi} \cdot ABF_i &= \frac{\pi}{1-\pi} \cdot ABF^I_i \cdot ABF^D_i \\
    &= \frac{\pi}{1-\pi} \sum_j X_{ij} \cdot E[\beta_j] \cdot ABF^D_i
\end{align*}

To extend these effector gene prioritization ideas to a more complete approach to gene and mechanism discovery for complex traits, several gaps remain. First, many (if not most) candidate genes do not lie within a GWAS locus, as do many genes “known” to be relevant to the trait. No existing method can statistically integrate association evidence for a gene outside of GWAS loci with its annotations to estimate the probability it is involved in the trait. Second, existing methods produce gene values comparable within a GWAS locus (i.e. via ranking of genes) but difficult to interpret across loci or traits.
