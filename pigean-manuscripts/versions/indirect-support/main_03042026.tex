\abstract{
Mapping the full set of genes relevant to human disease---not just those carrying detectable genetic associations---is critical for drug development and biological discovery, yet no principled framework exists for distinguishing gene-trait relevance from gene-trait association at scale. Here, we introduce PIGEAN (Priors Inferred from GEne ANnotations), a Bayesian framework that separately quantifies \textit{direct genetic support} (the probability that a gene is disease-associated based on genetic data) and \textit{indirect genetic support} (the probability that a gene is disease-relevant based on shared functional annotations with associated genes). By using unbiased human genetic associations as an anchor, PIGEAN calibrates {{NUM_TOTAL_GENE_ANNOTATIONS:,}} heterogeneous gene annotations---spanning knockout mouse phenotypes, curated pathways, literature co-occurrence, protein interactions, and single-cell expression---and learns which non-genetic evidence sources are informative for each trait while regularizing away noise and redundancy. Applied across {{NUM_TOTAL_TRAITS_MOUSE_MSIGDB:,}} traits ({{NUM_COMMON_TRAITS_MOUSE_MSIGDB:,}} common, {{NUM_RARE_TRAITS_MOUSE_MSIGDB:,}} rare), we produce {{NUM_GENETRAIT_REL_PP50:,}} calibrated gene-trait relationships (posterior probability $>$50\%), orders of magnitude denser than existing curated resources. We validate indirect support on three independent axes: it predicts held-out genetic associations in leave-one-chromosome-out and temporal analyses, prioritizes drug targets with higher clinical success rates than Open Targets, and enriches for causal genes in rare disease patients. PIGEAN provides a principled replacement for ad hoc gene prioritization and a dense probabilistic map of gene-trait relevance across the phenome.
}
